# Style: Insights

Analytical observer voice. Confident, data-backed, reads like someone studied the subject and is presenting findings. Reconstructed from the Claude Code Insights report writing style.

## Argument Structure

Every paragraph is a mini-argument with three beats:

1. **Claim**: Open with a characterization or bold assertion
2. **Evidence**: Back it up with specific data, examples, or observations woven into the sentence
3. **Conclusion**: Draw an insight or pattern from the evidence

Never list data separately. Always weave numbers and specifics into prose:

- Bad: "There were 177 sessions. 25 days were used. Most goals were UI-related."
- Good: "With 177 sessions over just 25 days and the vast majority of goals being UI-related, the pattern is clear."

## Sentence Structure

- Longer sentences than conversational writing, with subclauses and parentheticals
- Parenthetical examples for quick context: "(styling fixes, visual refinements, layout adjustments)"
- Contrast structure: "Despite X, Y" / "not X but Y" / "while X, Y"
- Em dashes for asides and pivots — they're part of this style
- Compound sentences that build momentum across a claim

## Characterization

- Open paragraphs or sections with a label: "You are a highly visual, iteration-driven developer"
- Use metaphors to crystallize concepts: "hands-on creative director", "pair programmer you're actively steering"
- **Bold** for key phrases that carry the argument — the words the reader should remember
- Second person ("you") when writing about someone, third person when writing about something

## Tone

- Confident. No hedging. Not "it seems like" but "your biggest pain point is clearly..."
- Signal words: "clearly", "consistently", "shows", "reflects", "demonstrates"
- Observational — like analyzing from the outside, not participating
- Data gives weight to conclusions, not opinions
- Specific: name exact tools, exact numbers, exact examples. Never "some" or "various".

## Closing Pattern

End with a key insight — one sentence that captures the entire narrative as a pattern:

- "You are a visual-first iterative builder who steers Claude through rapid feedback loops on UI polish, rejecting wrong approaches quickly until the pixel-level details match your mental model."

## Anti-patterns

- No bullet point lists in the main text. Flowing prose only.
- No hedging: avoid "might", "perhaps", "it seems", "possibly"
- No filler: avoid "it's worth noting", "interestingly", "it should be mentioned"
- No generic praise: avoid "impressive", "great job", "well done"
- No instructions or advice unless the format explicitly calls for it

## Examples

**Bad**: "The project had many commits. The developer was productive and used good practices. They focused on UI work mostly."

**Good**: "Despite the friction, the output is undeniable — **121 commits in 25 days** with a 90% goal achievement rate shows someone who pushes through frustrations and ships consistently. The energy is concentrated on pixel-perfect polish rather than architecture, though occasional infrastructure work (like the VPS cron migration) reveals broader range."

**Bad**: "You are good at many things including frontend and backend development."

**Good**: "Your interaction style is that of a **hands-on creative director** who treats the codebase as a pair programming session you're actively steering — 519 check-ins suggest a pattern of incremental feedback loops rather than upfront specification."
